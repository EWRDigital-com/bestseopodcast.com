How to Craft the Perfect SEO Title Tag (Our 4-Step Process) by Joshua Hardwick #409

Ep. 40944 min2018-03-19

Crafting the perfect SEO title tag follows a four-step process: find a primary keyword theme, add long-tail variations, draft a concise 50 to 60 character title, then refine it with emotion, brackets, curiosity, and front-loaded keywords.

Apple PodcastsSpotifyYouTubeiHeartRadioRSS
Key takeaways
  • Keep title tags between 50 and 60 characters to avoid truncation on the search engine result page.
  • Front-load your primary keyword in the title tag, as it catches attention and may produce keyword-rich inbound links.
  • Add emotion, brackets, or a curiosity gap to increase click-through rate once you rank in the top 10.
  • Target one primary keyword theme and weave in long-tail variations naturally, without unnatural shoehorning.
  • Google may rewrite your title tag if it thinks a better one exists, so optimize proactively but do not stress over it.
Long tail keywords are the search queries with very low individual search volume, but an enormous total search demand as a group.

Questions this episode answers

How long should an SEO title tag be?

According to the article discussed in this episode, a title tag should be 50 to 60 characters. Going beyond that risks the title being cut off with a trailing ellipsis on the search engine result page, which creates a poor user experience.

What is the difference between a title tag and an H1 tag?

They are in fact different elements. The title tag appears in the browser tab and on the Google search result page. The H1 tag is the visible heading on the page itself. In platforms like WordPress they are often pulled from the same source, but they can be set independently.

Why does Google sometimes rewrite my title tag?

Google may rewrite a title tag if it thinks the tag is poor, if a more suitable title exists for a given query, or if anchor text from inbound links suggests a different topic. If no title tag exists at all, Google will create one on its own.

Terms defined in this episode
Title Tag
An HTML element whose primary job is to tell visitors and search engines what they can expect from the web page in the shortest and most concise way possible. It appears in the browser tab and on the search engine result page.
Long Tail Keywords
Search queries with very low individual search volume but an enormous total search demand as a group. The name comes from the long tail of the search demand curve, a graph that plots all keywords by their search volumes.
Curiosity Gap
A title tag technique that teases information the reader needs to know, compelling them to click through. The episode cautions that the gap must be honest and not mislead the reader.
Front Loading
Placing the primary keyword near the beginning of the title tag so it catches attention quickly, may serve as a ranking factor, and can result in keyword-rich inbound links when other sites use the title as anchor text.
