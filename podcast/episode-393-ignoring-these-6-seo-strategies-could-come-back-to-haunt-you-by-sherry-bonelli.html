Questions this episode answers

What is the main SEO tip from episode 393 of the SEO Podcast?

The main tip is to regularly review pages on your site that receive high traffic and improve them by adding calls to action and interactive elements. The hosts say this follows the 80-20 rule and directly increases conversions, especially for new clients whose top pages often lack any conversion prompts.

Who wrote the article discussed in episode 393?

The article discussed is by Sherry Bonelli. The hosts reference it as a Halloween-themed piece titled about six SEO strategies that could come back to haunt you, though they do not fully walk through all six strategies within the transcript content available.

What are Shikos as mentioned in this podcast episode?

Shikos is the term the hosts use to mean shares, likes, and follows across social platforms. They run a weekly contest requiring 10 Shikos plus one review to avoid a penalty, tracking engagement on Facebook, Twitter, Instagram, YouTube, and LinkedIn.

Where can listeners find eWebResults on social media and review platforms?

Listeners can find eWebResults on Facebook, Twitter, Instagram, YouTube, and LinkedIn all under the handle eWebResults. Reviews can be left on iTunes, Stitcher via ewebresults.com slash Stitcher, Google My Business via ewebresults.com slash G+, and Yelp via ewebresults.com slash Yelp.

we always find clients, new clients who come to us who have pages that get lots of traffic. When we go and visit those pages, those pages don't have calls to action, don't have things to interact with.

Terms defined in this episode
Shikos
A coined term used by the hosts meaning shares, likes, and follows across social media platforms, used to measure weekly audience engagement.
80-20 rule (SEO context)
The principle that a small number of pages drive the majority of a site's traffic, so improving those pages yields the greatest gains in conversions.
Call to action
An element on a webpage prompting visitors to take a specific next step such as subscribing or inquiring, which the hosts say is often missing from high-traffic client pages.
