SEO agencies should handle both site management and marketing together, while avoiding tactics like traffic exchanges, over-optimization, and failing to treat every optimized page as a potential conversion page.

Key takeaways
  • Traffic exchanges generate visits under 30 seconds, create high bounce rates, and send untargeted traffic, making them a poor SEO investment.
  • Site optimization targets multiple phrases across the whole domain, while page optimization focuses each individual page on specific target phrases.
  • Over-optimization penalties come from combining low-value content with excessive headers, images, videos, and keyword stuffing on the same page.

Questions this episode answers

How do I fix a calendar plugin causing thousands of 404 errors in Google Webmaster Tools?

Update your robots.txt file to disallow the entire calendar directory using a wildcard, written as forward slash calendar asterisk forward slash, rather than targeting the specific action parameter. This blocks all calendar-related URLs from indexing and should clear the 404 errors after you resubmit.

Are traffic exchanges worth using for SEO?

No. Traffic exchanges are considered an old tactic that generates visits lasting less than 30 seconds, produces a high bounce rate, and delivers untargeted traffic. If you must test one, the hosts recommend using a separate splash page on a different domain name to keep your primary domain stats clean.

What is the difference between site optimization and page optimization?

Site optimization targets multiple keyword phrases across the entire domain, while page optimization focuses a single page on one set of specific target phrases. You cannot optimize your whole site for every phrase, so individual pages must each be optimized for their own target terms and kept consistent with the overall site.

Should every page on a website be built to convert visitors?

According to the hosts, every page that has been SEO optimized should be built to potentially convert visitors. Exceptions include pages like Terms of Service or Privacy Policy, which should likely be blocked in robots.txt. Even FAQ pages should include a form, a strong call to action, and impactful graphics since they can rank and drive traffic.

Terms defined in this episode
Over-optimization penalty
A penalty resulting from combining low-value or unengaging content with excessive headers, videos, images, and keyword-stuffed text on a single page, making all the effort put into that page worthless.
Competitive footprint
The defined set of keywords and topics a company should stay within when publishing content, ensuring all contributors target consistent phrases and do not dilute ranking potential by straying into unrelated areas.
CVO (search engine visitor optimization)
What you do after the visitor arrives on a page, focused on converting that traffic rather than just attracting it.
Traffic exchange
An old tactic where websites trade visits with each other, typically generating database-driven dynamic visits that last less than 30 seconds and result in high bounce rates with no targeted traffic.
