Key takeaways
  • Track lift-based revenue changes tied to ranking shifts or AI mentions, not just traffic or click counts.
  • Use Google Analytics, rank tracking tools, and business financials together to match visibility changes to revenue outcomes.
  • AI-generated content produces average output because it predicts words probabilistically and cannot build a narrative.
  • Build an AI sandwich on human bread: human ideas in, AI generates more ideas, human creates the final product.
Fire your SEO if your revenue is dropping.

About this episode

SEO veteran Ian Lurie joins Matthew Bertram to discuss navigating today's chaotic digital marketing landscape and why traditional metrics are becoming less reliable. They explore how the decline in click data requires a shift to lift-based measurement that correlates visibility improvements with revenue changes rather than traffic alone. • Marketers must focus on business performance over vanity metrics •…

Questions this episode answers

How should SEOs prove their value when traffic is declining?

Ian Lurie recommends lift-based measurement: match the timing of ranking gains or new AI mentions to changes in revenue. If rankings improved and revenue rose shortly after, that correlation makes the case. Rank tracking tools, Google Analytics, and the business's own financials are the three data sources to align.

What tools does Ian Lurie use to track AI and search visibility?

Lurie uses Google Analytics for traffic, Gumshoe to track AI mentions, and tools like SEMrush, Ahrefs, or Moz for rankings. He also built a custom Gmail MCP server integration in Claude to summarize alerts from roughly ten monitoring tools into a daily priority digest.

Why is AI-generated content a problem for marketers?

According to Lurie, AI generates content based on an average of existing material, so the output is inherently average. AI is probabilistic and predicts the next word, meaning it cannot build a narrative or guide a reader from one idea to the next the way humans do, which limits its ability to compel audiences.

What are Ian Lurie's two rules of marketing for smaller brands?

His first rule is maximize return on time invested, executing well in every customer interaction. His second rule is teach the shit out of everything, giving customers the information they need to make decisions without gating content or forcing them to contact you, which builds a dedicated audience even against large competitors.

In their words

You should build an AI sandwich on human bread. So it should go human comes up with ideas to give to the AI, AI generates more ideas, human generates the final product.

Terms defined in this episode
Lift-based measurement
Matching a specific change in earned media visibility, such as a ranking gain or new AI mention, to a corresponding rise or fall in revenue over time, rather than using time-matched or last-click attribution.
Generative engines
A term Lurie uses to describe AI systems like ChatGPT that generate responses and surface brand mentions, treated alongside traditional search engines as earned media channels to track.
Holdout testing
A method of turning off or reducing one marketing channel for a period to measure its isolated impact on business performance, typically reserved for paid channels rather than earned media like SEO.
User neutral rankings
Rank tracking results gathered in a way that removes personalization and location bias as much as possible, giving the most neutral view of a site's search position across all users.
