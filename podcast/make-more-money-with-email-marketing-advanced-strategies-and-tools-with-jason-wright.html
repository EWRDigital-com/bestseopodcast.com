Key takeaways
  • Email design does not matter; consistency and authenticity drive opens and conversions far more than visuals.
  • Proper DNS record setup is now required for deliverability, without it email marketing will not work.
  • Send newsletters two to four times per month; once a month risks being forgotten, more than once a week feels excessive.
  • Build the back-end email automation first, then worry about traffic and front-end pages.
  • MailChimp is the best starting point for beginners; ActiveCampaign is stronger for automation, CRM, and lead scoring.

Questions this episode answers

What email marketing platform should a beginner start with?

Jason Wright recommends starting with MailChimp because it is easy to use and fast to set up, even within a single day. ActiveCampaign is better for those who want more advanced automation, CRM features, and lead scoring. HubSpot is powerful but becomes very expensive as lists grow.

How often should you email your list?

Jason Wright says two to four times per month is the right range for most businesses. Once a month risks people forgetting who you are, and more than once a week feels overwhelming. Daily emails can work if your personality supports that level of authentic engagement.

Why is email deliverability harder now and how do you fix it?

DNS records used to be optional but are now required for inbox placement. All major email providers explain exactly which DNS records to configure. Skipping this step means emails will not reach inboxes and the marketing effort will fail, according to Jason Wright.

How should you evaluate an email marketing agency or consultant before hiring them?

Jason Wright advises getting them on a video call, asking questions, and watching for overselling. Anyone who guarantees specific results is a red flag. Look for someone with real years of experience, a track record with actual clients, and honesty about whether your business is a good fit for their services.

Terms defined in this episode
DNS records for email
Configuration settings now required by major email providers to improve deliverability and ensure emails reach inboxes rather than spam folders.
Lead magnet
An offer, such as a video series or downloadable, used to collect a name and email address; Jason Wright says it should carry a perceived value of a couple hundred dollars or more.
7-11-4 formula
A framework citing a Google study that says prospects need seven hours of content, eleven touchpoints, and exposure across four different channels before they are likely to trust and buy.
Back-end automation
The email nurture sequence and follow-up system that runs after a prospect joins a list or gets off a call; Jason Wright argues this should be built before traffic or front-end pages.
