Google’s E-A-T: Busting 10 of the Biggest Misconceptions by Lily Ray #503

Ep. 50330 min2020-12-17

Google's E-A-T stands for expertise, authoritativeness, and trustworthiness. It is not an algorithm, a direct ranking factor, or a quick fix, but a set of conceptual signals that influence search quality over time.

Apple PodcastsSpotifyYouTubeiHeartRadioRSS
Key takeaways
  • E-A-T is not an algorithm or a direct ranking factor, and there is no internal E-A-T score according to Google.
  • YMYL sites covering health, finance, or e-commerce face the highest E-A-T expectations from Google's quality raters.
  • Improving E-A-T takes time and does not produce immediate ranking gains the way technical fixes like site speed do.
  • E-A-T should be added on top of existing SEO work, not used as a replacement for technical SEO or other ranking efforts.
EAT is not a direct ranking factor. So expertise, authoritativeness and trustworthiness are also not individual ranking factors.

Questions this episode answers

Is E-A-T a Google ranking factor?

No. According to the episode, Google's Danny Sullivan confirmed there is no technical E-A-T ranking factor that can be measured directly like page speed. E-A-T is a set of conceptual signals that quality raters use to evaluate content, not a single measurable score.

Does adding an author biography directly improve rankings?

Not by itself. The episode says Google cannot recognize every author, and quality raters look at what the biography actually says and whether the author has off-site mentions from authoritative sources. The entity behind the author matters more than simply having a byline.

When was E-A-T first introduced by Google?

E-A-T was first introduced in the 2014 version of the Google Quality Guidelines, according to the episode. It was not created with the August 1, 2018 core algorithm update, which was informally named Medic by Barry Schwartz, not officially named by Google.

Terms defined in this episode
E-A-T
Expertise, authoritativeness, and trustworthiness. A set of conceptual signals Google quality raters use to evaluate content quality, not a direct ranking factor or a single algorithm.
YMYL
Your Money or Your Life. Topics that can directly impact a reader's happiness, health, financial success, or well-being, including e-commerce sites that collect credit card information.
Quality Raters
Human reviewers hired by Google who use the Quality Guidelines to evaluate search results. The episode notes there are approximately 20,000 quality raters and the guidelines are roughly 160 pages long.
Core Algorithm Update
Broad changes Google makes to its ranking systems. According to the episode, all such updates are called core updates and happen in real time, with the August 1, 2018 update informally nicknamed Medic by Barry Schwartz.
