The 10 Most Harmful Mobile SEO Mistakes by Aleh Barysevich #370

Ep. 37045 min2017-04-26

The 10 most harmful mobile SEO mistakes include slow site speed, blocked files, interstitial ads, unplayable content, bad redirects, mobile-only 404s, missing rich snippets, no viewport meta tag, poor mobile design, and not cross-checking metrics.

Apple PodcastsSpotifyYouTubeiHeartRadioRSS
Key takeaways
  • 53% of users abandon a page that takes longer than three seconds to load, so aim for under one second on mobile.
  • Blocking JavaScript, CSS, or image files from Googlebot can lower your mobile rankings.
  • Interstitial ads that cover the full screen are a poor mobile experience and can hurt rankings.
  • Responsive design eliminates redirect errors, mobile-only 404s, and cross-linking problems common on separate mobile and desktop sites.
  • Cross-check analytics tools because server-based stats like GoDaddy's count bots and every file hit, inflating numbers versus Google Analytics.
53% of people will abandon a page if it takes longer than three seconds to load.

About this episode

Avoiding these 10 mobile SEO mistakes will earn you better positions in the SERPs, drive more traffic to your mobile site, and keep your visitors happy.

Questions this episode answers

What is the most common mobile SEO mistake?

According to the article discussed in this episode, slow site speed is the number one mobile SEO mistake. Google research cited in the episode shows 53% of users abandon a page that takes longer than three seconds to load, and the recommended goal is under one second for mobile pages.

Why are interstitial ads bad for mobile SEO?

Interstitial ads that cover a page's main content create a poor user experience on mobile devices. The episode explains that pop-ups covering the screen, whether triggered on page load or by exit intent, can harm rankings. Google penalizes sites where content is obscured by full-screen overlays.

How does responsive design help avoid mobile SEO mistakes?

A responsive site uses one URL and one set of pages for both desktop and mobile, eliminating faulty redirects, mobile-only 404 errors, and cross-linking issues. The hosts recommend responsive design throughout the episode as the practical fix for most URL and content parity problems.

Why do Google Analytics and GoDaddy analytics show different traffic numbers?

Google Analytics filters out bots and spam traffic, while server-based analytics like GoDaddy's count every hit including bots and individual file requests. In the example from this episode, GoDaddy reported roughly 10,000 visits while Google Analytics showed only about 1,500 real visits to the same site.

Terms defined in this episode
Interstitial ad
A pop-up or overlay that covers a page's main content on a mobile device, which Google penalizes because it blocks users from accessing the content they searched for.
Mobile viewport meta tag
An HTML meta tag that tells the browser how to scale and fit a page to a specific screen size, preventing pages from appearing improperly fitted on mobile devices.
Rich snippets
Structured data markup using schema.org that helps Google display rich, accurate, and instant answers in search results, giving sites a leg up in mobile search.
Caching
The process of storing downloaded assets like images and logos on a user's device so subsequent pages do not need to re-download the same files, speeding up page load times.
