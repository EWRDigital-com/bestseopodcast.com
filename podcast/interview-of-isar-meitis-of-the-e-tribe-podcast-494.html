Questions this episode answers

Why does Isar Meitis prefer revenue-share deals over monthly retainers?

He argues that short-term retainers create misaligned incentives. A consultant on a six-month retainer spends the last three months hunting the next client instead of serving the current one. Revenue share and equity tie compensation directly to results, with no upper cap on earnings if the work succeeds.

How can a podcast be used as a B2B sales tool?

Meitis built a business around this idea. Inviting a target client as a podcast guest removes the cold-sales barrier entirely. The CEO gets an informal, hour-long conversation, relationships form naturally, and structured follow-up automation converts those conversations into clients at a much higher rate than traditional outreach.

What is the SEO value of a podcast transcript?

Matt Bertram explains that raw back-and-forth transcripts are not enough. Converting a transcript into a keyword-targeted article with proper H1 and H2 structure is what Google rewards. Meitis confirmed this approach grew his organic traffic from zero to 30 to 40 visitors a day in four to five months.

What should agency owners check before taking on a new client?

Both hosts warn that clients must be able to handle increased demand before marketing is scaled. One remodeler only did five houses a year and became overwhelmed when SEO ranked him across a wide region. Asking whether a business has the operational capacity to fulfill new leads is a critical step before starting work.

Terms defined in this episode
Revenue share
A deal structure where the consultant or marketer receives a percentage of sales generated rather than a fixed fee, aligning payment directly with results and removing any upper earnings cap.
Fractional CMO
A part-time or contract Chief Marketing Officer brought into a company to direct marketing strategy, as described by Matt Bertram in his oil-and-gas deal.
Social splintering
The practice of taking one piece of content such as a podcast episode and distributing it across multiple channels as short clips, soundbites, infographics, and articles to maximize reach.
Productizing services
Turning custom, project-based work into standardized, repeatable offerings so a business can scale without proportionally increasing headcount or complexity.
