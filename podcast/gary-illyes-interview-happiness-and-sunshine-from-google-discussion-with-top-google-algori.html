Questions this episode answers

Is EAT a Google ranking signal?

No. Gary Illyes states directly that EAT is not a ranking signal. It is a framework written for human quality raters to explain what Google algorithms look for. The best source to understand EAT is the publicly available raters guidelines document, which he describes as over 150 pages long.

Do you need links to rank in Google?

Not always, but links matter more in less mature language markets. Gary Illyes explains that on the English-language internet Google understands the web well enough that pages can rank without external links. In other language areas, a lack of links creates a data void that makes ranking much harder.

How can small businesses compete with big brands in Google search?

Gary Illyes recommends specializing in one thing and doing it exceptionally well, optimizing images so the business appears in image search results, and creating a painless purchase experience. He uses a small Zurich bakery that specialized in croissants and offered fast bike delivery as a real example.

What changed with Google's no-follow link treatment?

Google shifted from treating no-follow as a directive to treating it as a hint, matching what most other search engines already did. This allows Google to discover harmful content such as spam or malware links that were previously invisible because a no-follow annotation hid them from the algorithm.

Terms defined in this episode
EAT (Expertise, Authoritativeness, Trustworthiness)
Not a ranking signal but a framework in Google's raters guidelines explaining to human quality raters what the algorithms look for when evaluating content quality.
LTCP (Language Demotion Country Promotion)
A Google signal that boosts search results geographically relevant to the searcher's location and demotes results in languages the searcher is unlikely to want.
No-follow as a hint
Google's updated treatment of the no-follow link attribute, changing it from a strict directive that blocked signal flow to a hint that Google may use or ignore, allowing discovery of spam and harmful links.
Site move tool
A feature in Google Search Console that transfers ranking signals from an old domain or site structure to a new one during a rebrand or migration, unless the move is flagged as spammy.
