Questions this episode answers

How does a sales background help with digital marketing strategy?

According to Matt Bertram, a sales background helps because marketing is essentially sales conducted online. Knowing the sales funnel, customer objections, and closing techniques lets marketers build content and targeting that moves prospects as far down the funnel as possible before anyone picks up the phone.

What is the most important first step in building a marketing strategy for a sales team?

Matt Bertram says the most important first step is defining the target persona and mapping the customer journey. Without knowing who you are going after and what that journey looks like, every channel choice, ad buy, and content piece is just shooting in the dark.

How can companies use customer lists for digital ad targeting?

Matt Bertram explains that customer email lists can be hashed so they match against Facebook, LinkedIn, or other accounts tied to those emails. This lets companies retarget known contacts with ads or build a lookalike audience from that data set to reach a broader, similar group.

Why should salespeople care about content and marketing materials?

Matt Bertram argues that sales teams need blog posts ranking for buyer questions, one-pagers, white papers, videos, and other assets at each stage of the sales cycle. Without those pieces, salespeople cannot efficiently move prospects along, and graphic design and marketing departments remain underutilized.

Terms defined in this episode
E-E-A-T
Google's framework of expertise, authoritativeness, trust, and experience used to evaluate content quality and site credibility, as mentioned by Matt Bertram in the context of publishing books and authoritative content.
Lookalike audience
A larger ad targeting group built by matching a customer email list to social media accounts and then finding new users who share similar characteristics, as described by Matt Bertram.
Sales enablement
The practice of equipping sales teams with content, automation, personalization, and retargeting tools to help them move prospects through the buying process more efficiently.
Query diversity
A Google behavior where differentiating from commonly stated viewpoints or adding more authoritative expert content can help a piece of content surface in search results, as described by Matt Bertram.
