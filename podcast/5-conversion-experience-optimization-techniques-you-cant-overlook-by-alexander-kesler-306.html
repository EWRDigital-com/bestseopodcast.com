Key takeaways
  • Google made Panda a permanent core ranking signal, so all content must be original, relevant, and non-thin.
  • For every field added to a form, you lose roughly 10% of form submissions, so keep forms short.
  • Adding a percentage progress bar starting at 40% complete can increase form submissions by 30%.
  • Separate landing pages with one image, one form, and one call to action work especially well for legal and high-priced services.

Questions this episode answers

What are the five conversion and experience optimization techniques from Alexander Kesler?

The five techniques are: optimize across all touch points, adopt a data-driven mentality and test, use multiple landing pages, do not let forms become a conversion killer by keeping them short, and reduce distractions by giving every marketing asset a single clear goal for the visitor.

How do you reduce form abandonment and increase form submissions?

Keep the number of fields as low as possible since each added field costs roughly 10% of submissions. Make the form visually compact using inline placeholder labels. Adding a progress bar that starts at 40% complete has been shown to produce a 30% increase in form submissions by signaling users they are nearly done.

What did Google do to the Panda algorithm and what does it mean for content creators?

Google made Panda a permanent part of its core ranking signal, meaning it runs continuously instead of in periodic refreshes. Content creators must ensure their content is original, relevant, not thin, and ideally includes multiple media types such as text of six to eight hundred words, videos, and images.

When should you use a separate landing page versus relying on your regular site pages?

A separate landing page with no distractions works best for high-priced products or industries like legal services, where stripping away menus and extra content and focusing on one form and one call to action drives more conversions. For faster service needs like plumbing, a well-optimized site page with visible forms and phone numbers can work just as well.

Terms defined in this episode
Experience Optimization
Understanding and controlling what users do when they reach your site across all interactions with a brand, both online and offline, to improve engagement and conversions.
SEVO
Search Engine Visitor Optimization, an earlier term the hosts used for the same concept of optimizing the experience of visitors who arrive from search engines.
Panda Algorithm
A Google algorithm update targeting low-quality pages with thin, duplicate, or bad content, which Google later made a permanent part of its core ranking signal so it runs continuously.
Touch Points
The different ways and devices through which people engage with a brand, including desktop, mobile, tablet, radio ads, and TV commercials, each requiring its own optimized experience.
