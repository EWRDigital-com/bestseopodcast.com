How to Target Multiple Cities Without Hurting Your SEO by Loren Baker #500

Ep. 50039 min2020-10-19

City pages can hurt SEO if they duplicate content, but done right with unique local content and proper Google My Business setup, they are a core local SEO strategy for targeting multiple cities.

Apple PodcastsSpotifyYouTubeiHeartRadioRSS
Key takeaways
  • Duplicate city pages that only swap out the city name can trigger Google Panda penalties.
  • City pages need unique, locally relevant content, such as hard water issues for plumbers or local plant info for florists.
  • Google My Business must use a real address, not a P.O. box, to avoid manual penalties.
  • Getting into the Google local three-pack requires a complete GMB profile, photos, and strong reviews.
Done right. City pages can be an integral part of your local SEO strategy.

Questions this episode answers

Can targeting multiple cities hurt your SEO?

Yes, if you duplicate city pages and only swap out the city name, Google can penalize the site. The fix is writing unique, locally relevant content for each important city page rather than copying the same page across all locations.

How important is Google My Business for local SEO?

The hosts call it essential, saying more than 50 percent of local searches result in a store visit. GMB must use a real address, not a P.O. box, and the profile should be fully completed with photos and reviews to improve map-pack rankings.

Terms defined in this episode
NAP citations
Name, address, and phone number listings across directories such as Yelp, TripAdvisor, and Bing that must stay consistent so Google can trust the information.
Google local map pack
The three coveted local business spots shown in Google search results, which the hosts call the holy grail of local SEO.
Doorway pages
Pages built to target specific keywords that funnel all visitors to a single conversion page, which carries a Google penalty.
Mobile-first indexing
Google's policy of using the mobile version of a site as the primary basis for ranking, meaning sites can rank differently on desktop versus mobile.
