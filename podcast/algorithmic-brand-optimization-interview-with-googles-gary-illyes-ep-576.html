Gary Illyes of Google confirms links remain important ranking signals but sites can rank without them, and that no-follow links are now treated as hints to help Google discover spam and low-link-density languages.

Key takeaways
  • Sites can rank without external links, especially on the mature English-language web, but links matter more in competitive or low-link-density language areas.
  • EAT is not a ranking signal itself; it is guidance written for human quality raters explaining what Google algorithms look for.
  • AMP helped start a web speed revolution by pushing publishers to optimize page load times, even those who never implemented AMP directly.

About this episode

Questions this episode answers

Does Google use no-follow links for ranking?

Gary Illyes says Google treats no-follow as a hint rather than a directive. This lets Google discover spam, malware, and content in low-link-density language areas it previously could not see. He does not confirm no-follow links are used directly in rankings but says it is a possibility.

Can a website rank on Google without any external links?

Yes, according to Gary Illyes. He cites examples of small brand sites with no external links ranking in top positions for commercial queries. He notes it is easier to rank without links on the mature English-language web, but harder in competitive niches or lower-resource language areas.

What is EAT and how does it affect Google rankings?

Gary Illyes says EAT is not a ranking signal. It is documentation written for human quality raters to explain what Google algorithms look for regarding content quality. He recommends reading the raters guidelines document and points to Marie Haynes, Glenn Gabe, and Jennifer Slagg as credible interpreters.

How can small businesses compete with big brands in Google search?

Gary Illyes recommends that small businesses specialize in a niche, optimize images so they appear in visual search, and create a distinctive brand experience that gets people talking. He uses a Zurich bakery that focused on croissants and offered fast delivery as a real example of this working.

Terms defined in this episode
No-follow hint
When Google treats a no-follow link attribute as a hint rather than a directive, meaning it can still crawl and use that link to discover spam, malware, or ranking signals rather than ignoring it entirely.
EAT
Expertise, Authoritativeness, and Trustworthiness. Gary Illyes describes it as guidance written for human quality raters explaining what Google algorithms look for, not a direct ranking signal.
LDCP
Language Demotion Country Promotion. A Google signal that boosts search results geographically relevant to the searcher and demotes results in languages they are unlikely to want.
Site move tool
A feature in Google Search Console that transfers all existing signals from an old domain or site structure to a new one when a site is rebranding or migrating, unless the move involves spammy activity.
