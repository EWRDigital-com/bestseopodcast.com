Smart Content & Smart SEO: Mastering Audiences & Architecture by Michael Kirchhoff #401

Key takeaways
  • Organize content into topical categories, subcategories, and tertiary categories, going at least three levels deep.
  • Use schema markup and breadcrumb links to help search engines determine the context of each article.
  • Add real entities such as related people, places, and things to build contextual authority and expand long tail keyword opportunities.
  • Place internal links higher on the page to signal their importance to Google and increase conversions.
  • Content aggregator pages tied to primary and secondary topical tags help build category-level contextual relevancy.

Questions this episode answers

What is smart content in SEO?

According to this episode, smart content is discoverable, optimized, profitable, and easily displayed with related topics and content. It links throughout the user's journey on a website, improving ranking, traffic, link equity, crawl paths for search engine indexation, and user engagement.

How should I organize content for better SEO using taxonomy?

The episode recommends organizing content into topical categories, subcategories, and tertiary categories, going three levels deep. For example, the top category might be renewables, the next wind power, and then wind power projects. This structure helps users and search engines navigate contextually related content.

Why are internal links important for smart content?

Internal links increase time on site, page views per visit, and conversions. The episode advises placing links higher on the page and within the content itself to signal their importance to Google. Using naked anchor text is recommended, and site-wide links should be avoided.

How do real entities like people, places, and things help SEO content?

Adding real entities such as related people, places, and things builds contextual authority and expands long tail keyword opportunities. For example, referencing Elon Musk in a renewable energy article or mentioning Shark Tank and Silicon Valley in a venture capital article can strengthen topical relevance.

Terms defined in this episode
Smart Content
Content that is discoverable, optimized, profitable, and easily displayed with related topics, linked throughout the user journey to improve ranking, traffic, link equity, crawl paths, and user engagement.
Taxonomy
The way content is organized into topical categories, subcategories, and tertiary categories, requiring collaboration across SEO, product, UX, UI, content, and leadership teams.
Keyword Clusters
Groups of related keywords assigned to each topic in a taxonomy, including long tail keywords, that reflect audience search behavior and expand a site's topic authority.
Content Aggregation
Creating pages that display content based on primary and secondary topical tags to build contextual relevancy at the category level, similar to the related post section on Search Engine Journal.
