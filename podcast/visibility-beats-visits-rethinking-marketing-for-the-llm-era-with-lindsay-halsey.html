Questions this episode answers

How can SEO agencies show clients what traffic is coming from AI tools like ChatGPT?

Lindsay Halsey recommends creating a custom channel in Google Analytics that groups traffic from ChatGPT, Gemini, Perplexity, and similar tools. It takes about five minutes to set up and applies retroactively, letting agencies show clients both the volume and the engagement quality of that AI-referred traffic.

What is an LLMs.txt file and why does it matter for SEO?

An LLMs.txt file tells AI crawlers which content on a site they are allowed to access. Without it, LLM bots may be blocked, limiting a site's visibility in AI-generated answers. Matt Bertram notes that over 50 percent of sites do not yet have one, and the file is distinct from robots.txt.

How should agencies change their content strategy when blog traffic is falling?

Lindsay Halsey suggests going back into top-performing blog posts and replacing generic newsletter sign-up calls-to-action with downloads that match what the visitor actually searched for, such as a proposal template. The goal is to convert a higher share of the smaller audience that still clicks through.

How is success being redefined in SEO reporting as AI search grows?

Lindsay Halsey says agencies should move away from measuring only sessions and start reporting on visibility, share of voice, and how often a brand appears in AI overviews. She compares this to how PR and paid media agencies have always reported on impressions and publication placements.

Terms defined in this episode
LLMs.txt
A file placed on a website to tell AI language model crawlers which content they may access, separate from robots.txt, intended to ensure the site is included when LLMs generate answers.
AI Overview
A Google search feature that displays an AI-generated summary at the top of results; Lindsay Halsey notes agencies can track how often a site appears in these using tools like Wincher.
Share of voice
A visibility metric measuring how often a brand appears in search or AI-generated results relative to competitors, used as an alternative to session-based reporting.
Custom channel in Google Analytics
A user-defined grouping in Google Analytics that segments traffic by source, used here to isolate visits arriving from AI tools such as ChatGPT, Gemini, and Perplexity.
