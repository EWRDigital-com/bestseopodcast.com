Questions this episode answers

Should I include my site name in every title tag?

It depends on how established your brand is. New companies should place their site name at the end of each title tag to build brand recognition alongside keywords. Well-known brands with solid rankings can skip it and use that space for additional keywords or a call to action.

Is it worth paying to submit my site to hundreds of directories for local SEO?

No. The hosts recommend limiting submissions to about 20 quality directories including Google My Business, Facebook, Bing Local, Yahoo Local, Waze, Chamber of Commerce, BBB, and relevant niche directories. Tools like Bright Local, Yext, or Moz Local handle this affordably without needing to pay a lot to a third party.

How should a local SEO landing page differ from a standard landing page?

A local landing page should include local imagery such as your building or recognizable local landmarks, and use locally familiar language. The hosts use the example of calling Houston's 610 freeway 'the loop' so that local visitors immediately recognize the content as relevant to their community.

Terms defined in this episode
Shiko
An eWebResults branded term meaning social engagement, specifically shares, likes, and follows across social media platforms.
Double opt-in
An email subscription method where users sign up and then click a confirmation link, resulting in higher open rates and better inbox delivery compared to single opt-in.
Google Three Pack
The set of three local business results Google displays for local searches, reduced from seven results, and later expanded to include local ads tagged in green.
Instant Leads
A pay-per-click program described by eWebResults designed to deliver leads within 24 hours by combining ad setup with a matching landing page experience.
