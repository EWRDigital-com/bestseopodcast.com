How Your Website Redesign Can Sabotage Your Paid Search PPC Efforts by Pauline Jakober #398

Ep. 39827 min2017-12-04

A website redesign can destroy paid search performance by removing phone numbers, trust symbols, tracking codes, thank you pages, forms, and HTTPS, so each element must be preserved or rebuilt carefully.

Apple PodcastsSpotifyYouTubeiHeartRadioRSS
Key takeaways
  • Removing the phone number during a redesign eliminates an easy conversion path for PPC traffic.
  • Trust symbols like BBB badges and testimonials should appear on every page visitors can land on.
  • Tracking codes including Google Analytics, remarketing, and AdWords conversion tags must survive any redesign.
  • Thank you pages enable specific remarketing, prevent visitor confusion, and capture conversion data.
  • Dropping HTTPS during a redesign triggers browser security warnings and can tank both rankings and conversions.

Questions this episode answers

What tracking codes should you protect during a website redesign for PPC?

The episode lists four codes to protect: the Google Analytics code, the remarketing code, the website call metrics code such as CallRail, and the AdWords conversion code. Facebook Pixel and LinkedIn tags are also mentioned as codes that should be retained.

Why do thank you pages matter for paid search campaigns?

Thank you pages serve three purposes according to the episode: they prevent visitors from being confused about whether a form was submitted, they create an opportunity to upsell or manage buyer remorse, and they are a prime location for tracking codes that enable specific remarketing to people who completed a conversion action.

How can form changes during a redesign hurt PPC conversion rates?

Replacing a form with a simple email link can cause visitors to leave the page when their mail client opens, losing the conversion. Also, making the form physically larger on the page can reduce completions even if the number of fields stays the same, because it looks like too much work.

What AMP considerations are critical when relaunching a site?

The episode advises making sure canonical links are on every AMP page, that proper structured data is on each page, and that the AMP HTML guidelines are followed regarding allowed JavaScript and CSS. Breaking or removing AMP during a redesign can cause a significant loss of traffic.

Terms defined in this episode
Remarketing code
A tracking tag placed on a website that allows advertisers to serve targeted ads to people who have previously visited specific pages, including thank you pages.
AMP HTML
A web page format designed for fast mobile loading that requires canonical links, structured data, and adherence to specific JavaScript and CSS guidelines on every page.
Trust symbols
Visual indicators on a website such as BBB badges, review badges, and testimonials that signal credibility to visitors and can positively impact conversions.
Google Tag Manager
A tool recommended in the episode to centrally manage and standardize tracking codes like Analytics, remarketing, and conversion tags across a website.
