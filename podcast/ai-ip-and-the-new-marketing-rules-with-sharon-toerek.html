AI, IP, And The New Marketing Rules With Sharon Toerek

  • Create a written AI policy, both internal and external facing, even if it starts as a skeleton outline.
  • Review and update contracts to specifically address AI usage, liability, and indemnification language.

Questions this episode answers

Who owns content created with generative AI?

According to Sharon Toerek, this is one of the top questions from marketing agency owners right now. Ownership is unsettled, and businesses should address it directly in contracts. Courts are still working through cases that will define the guardrails around AI-generated content and who bears responsibility if infringement occurs.

What should businesses do right now to protect themselves legally from AI risks?

Sharon Toerek recommends three steps: have conversations with clients, vendors, and contractors about their AI use practices; create a written AI policy for internal and external audiences; and update contracts to specifically address AI usage and liability. She also suggests reviewing insurance coverage, especially cyber and umbrella policies.

Is there insurance that covers AI-related liability?

As of this episode, no dedicated AI liability insurance products exist. Sharon Toerek says cyber insurance is the closest product that could potentially evolve to cover AI situations. Businesses are advised to review their existing insurance stack and consider carrying more coverage than they did a few years ago.

How does copyright infringement analysis work when AI appropriates content?

Sharon Toerek explains courts weigh multiple factors, not just the percentage of content taken. Courts consider how much was taken, whether it was the core of the work, and whether it impacts the market for the original. She notes most creators lack the financial means to go through the full forensic process required to prove a claim.

Terms defined in this episode
Trade secret protection
A form of IP protection suited for proprietary content that is not published publicly, protecting the unique secret sauce of a business rather than registered copyright.
IP triangle
Sharon Toerek's framework for analyzing a business's intellectual property across three points: brand protection via trademark, content protection via copyright or trade secret, and transactions that monetize the IP.
State patchwork of data privacy laws
The US approach to data privacy regulation, where individual states each enact their own rules rather than following a single federal standard, similar to what is now emerging for AI regulation.
Substantial similarity
A legal standard used in copyright infringement cases requiring a creator to prove that the allegedly infringing work is substantially similar to the original, alongside proving the infringer had access to the original work.
