7 Email Marketing Mistakes You're Making by Reshu Rathi #368

Ep. 36840 min2017-04-12

Avoid email marketing failure by scrubbing your list before sending, writing strong subject lines, segmenting your audience, offering real value, and timing sends to match your site's peak traffic hours.

Key takeaways
  • Scrub your email list before any campaign to prevent bounces, spam flags, and deliverability damage to your domain.
  • Treat email subject lines like page titles and aim for seven words to boost open rates by up to 203%.
  • Segment your list so subscribers receive emails relevant to their specific interests, not one generic message.
  • Match your email send time to the peak traffic hours shown in your website analytics.
  • Avoid purely promotional emails by including free educational value so subscribers think of you when ready to buy.

Questions this episode answers

What is the most important email marketing mistake not mentioned in most articles?

Scrubbing your email list before sending is described as foundational and more important than any of the seven listed mistakes. Sending to unclean lists with bad addresses or unsubscribes causes bounces, spam flags, and can get your domain penalized so even legitimate emails land in spam folders.

How should you choose the best time to send an email campaign?

Check your website analytics to find the time of day you get the most site visits. Sending your email campaign at or just before that peak time means your audience is already online and interested in what you offer, which should increase engagement rates.

When is it acceptable to include multiple calls to action in one email?

Multiple CTAs work fine in a general newsletter touching a broad list, as long as the email is short and easy to read. However, when promoting a specific product, service, or event aggressively, the email should focus on one single call to action only.

How do you make an email subject line feel personal without using the recipient's first name?

Reference the subscriber's known interests or preferences directly in the subject line. The example given is Champs sending a subject like 'Your new Jordans are about to release,' which uses 'your' and targets a specific product interest without inserting a name.
