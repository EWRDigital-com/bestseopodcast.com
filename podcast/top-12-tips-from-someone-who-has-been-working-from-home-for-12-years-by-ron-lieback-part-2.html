Working from home productively requires scheduled breaks, a comfortable workspace, mastering team technology, setting boundaries with kids, and staying social, as outlined in tips 7 through 12 from Ron Lieback's 12-year work-from-home experience.

Key takeaways
  • Taking regular breaks, such as 10 minutes every hour, prevents physical strain and improves focus during work-from-home sessions.
  • A standing desk can reduce shoulder knots and back pain for people who otherwise sit hunched over a computer all day.
  • Setting team expectations about your schedule reduces interruptions and prevents the productivity loss caused by constant multitasking.
  • Even introverts benefit from social interaction, so scheduling video hangouts with friends or colleagues helps prevent burnout.
  • Mastering your team's technology platform, such as Trello, Slack, or Teamwork, is essential so colleagues have visibility into your work.

Questions this episode answers

What are the last six of Ron Lieback's 12 tips for working from home?

Tips 7 through 12 are: schedule breaks to rest your eyes and body, create a comfortable and quiet workspace suited to each task, set expectations with your team about your schedule, socialize regularly even via video hangouts, master the technology your team uses, and set boundaries when working around kids.

How important is socializing when working from home full time?

The hosts cite a study showing that both introverts and extroverts reported equal happiness after social interactions, suggesting everyone needs connection. Ron Lieback uses Google Hangouts with close friends to replace the weekly in-person drinks he can no longer have.

What technology tools do the hosts use for remote team communication?

The hosts mention having used Slack previously and currently using Teamwork, which they say works really well for their agency. They also reference HubSpot for CRM and note that most clients using HubSpot only use a small fraction of its capabilities.

How should business owners respond to leads coming in during a slow market?

The hosts describe a client who was texting leads a simple call me message up to 36 hours after receiving the lead, with no follow-up process. They recommend calling quickly, leaving a message, and using email automation and remarketing to nurture leads who do not respond immediately.
