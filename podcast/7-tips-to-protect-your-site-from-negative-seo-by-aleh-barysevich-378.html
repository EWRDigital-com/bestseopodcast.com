7 Tips to Protect Your Site From Negative SEO by Aleh Barysevich #378

Ep. 37847 min2017-06-20

Negative SEO attacks via spammy links, fake reviews, content scraping, and hacking can tank your rankings, and seven defensive practices including link audits, site speed monitoring, and security upgrades can protect your site.

Key takeaways
  • Run regular link audits using Moz Open Site Explorer and Google Search Console to catch suspicious link spikes or drops early.
  • Resubmit new content to Google immediately after publishing to claim it before scrapers can get it indexed first.
  • Monitor Google My Business and Yelp for sudden floods of one-star reviews, flag fake ones, and report them to the platform.
  • Watch your keyword click-through rate in Google Analytics; an unexplained drop could signal someone is artificially inflating your bounce rate.
  • Keep WordPress, plugins, and themes updated and move your site to SSL to close the security holes hackers exploit for negative SEO.

Questions this episode answers

What is negative SEO and who does it?

Negative SEO is using black hat techniques against another site, typically carried out by a disgruntled competitor or their associates, with the goal of reducing that site's search rankings through tactics like spammy links, fake reviews, content scraping, or outright hacking.

How do you find and remove bad links pointing to your site?

Use Moz Open Site Explorer and Google Search Console to export your link profile and compare results. Review suspicious links, and if they come from low-quality sites, disavow them in Google Search Console and email the webmaster asking them to remove the link.

Can scraped content hurt your SEO rankings?

Yes. If a competitor scrapes your content and gets it indexed before you do, Google may treat your page as the duplicate and devalue it. The fix is to resubmit content to Google immediately after publishing and keep your sitemap current.

What should you do if you get flooded with fake negative reviews on Google My Business?

Flag and report the reviews as fictitious directly within Google My Business and let Google handle removal. Do the same on Yelp. Fake reviews often have no profile photo, were created recently, or show a pattern of only negative reviews across a profile.

Terms defined in this episode
Negative SEO
The practice of implementing black hat SEO techniques on another site, usually by a competitor, to reduce that site's search engine rankings through spammy links, fake reviews, content scraping, or hacking.
Forceful crawling
A negative SEO attack where someone causes excessive bot traffic to hit a target site, creating a heavy server load that slows down or crashes the site.
Disavow
Telling Google you do not want certain inbound links associated with your site, used when spammy or low-quality links are pointing to you and harming your rankings.
