Questions this episode answers

How do social signals affect SEO rankings?

Social signals like the number of Facebook likes on a brand page, Facebook shares, Twitter followers, and tweets mentioning your brand or linking to your site are direct ranking factors. Indirect effects include increased inbound links, positive reviews on Google Local and Yelp, and lower bounce rates from socially familiar visitors.

Should I use URL shorteners when sharing links on Twitter for SEO purposes?

Avoid URL shorteners when tweeting links if possible. When your tweet gets retweeted, a full direct link passes real link value back to your site. A shortened URL does not. Only shorten when a keyword-rich blog title makes the full URL too long to preserve characters.

What social media pages should a business set up for SEO?

At minimum, set up a Facebook page, a Twitter page, and Google Plus. For Google Plus specifically, create a personal author page, a Google Plus Local page for the business, and a business Plus page. All three serve different but complementary SEO purposes.

Is a high bounce rate always bad for SEO?

Not always. If a business's primary conversion is a phone call, a visitor who lands on the site, reads the strong call to action, calls immediately, and then leaves has converted successfully. In that scenario a high or even 100 percent bounce rate can reflect a well-performing page.

Terms defined in this episode
Social signals
Metrics from social platforms, such as Facebook likes, shares, Twitter followers, and brand mentions, that Google uses as indicators of a site's credibility and relevance for ranking purposes.
Relative author tag
An HTML link attribute using rel equals author that connects a piece of content to a writer's Google Plus personal page, enabling an author image to appear in search results.
Exact match domain (EMD)
A domain name that exactly matches a target keyword phrase, such as carbonfiberhoods.com, which can be a ranking factor but requires good content and products to avoid negative effects.
Google Plus Local
A Google Plus page type tied to a physical business location, distinct from a personal profile page and a general business Plus page, used to improve local search visibility.
