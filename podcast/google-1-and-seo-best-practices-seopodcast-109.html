Episode33 min2011-07-18

Google Plus One rolled out on websites this week but the hosts doubt it will catch on, while the episode covers four social media mistakes beginners make and best practices for growing a social presence.

Apple PodcastsSpotifyYouTubeiHeartRadioRSS
Key takeaways
  • The four beginner social media mistakes are spamming, not updating frequently, not promoting your accounts, and only posting about yourself.
  • Cross-linking social accounts to your website and putting social handles on business cards and invoices drives real follower growth.
  • Foursquare check-in discounts give users a clear reason to check in, which is a model any local business can apply.
  • 60% of social media posts contain a link, and including links in updates helps drive traffic and can support SEO.

Questions this episode answers

Did Google Plus One launch on websites and will it succeed?

The episode says Google Plus One rolled out on websites around the 31st or 1st of the week recorded. The hosts are skeptical it will catch on because Google has no strong social platform behind it and has failed at previous social efforts like Google Buzz and Wave.

How should businesses give people a reason to check in on Foursquare?

The episode recommends offering a concrete reward, such as a free item or discount after a set number of check-ins, so customers understand why checking in benefits them. One host describes offering a free DVD to anyone who checks in three consecutive times at their church.

How can businesses grow their social media following?

The hosts say businesses should cross-link all social accounts on their website, put Facebook and Twitter handles on invoices and business cards, and clearly communicate to customers why they should follow the account, such as receiving tips, discounts, or exclusive offers.

Google's My Client Center, a tool that lets agencies manage multiple pay-per-click accounts from one place.

A group on Twitter whose members tag their profiles with TFB, meaning they will automatically follow back anyone who follows them, used to inflate follower counts.
