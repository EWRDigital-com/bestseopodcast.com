Key takeaways
  • Google Boost ads offer no custom keyword selection, only preset category tags, limiting advertiser control.
  • Creating comparison pages for competitor brand terms is a legitimate SEO tactic that builds relevant content.
  • Static, keyword-rich URLs help Google index and rank pages more effectively than database-string URLs.
  • CMS choice should match your coding skill level: ModX for coders, WordPress for non-coders.
  • 301 redirecting old URL structures preserves link juice when changing site architecture, avoiding duplicate content penalties.

Questions this episode answers

What CMS features are most important for SEO?

The episode highlights static keyword-rich URLs, individually customizable title tags and H1 tags, RSS feeds, canonical tags or 301 redirects for duplicate content, and multi-level categorization. WordPress, ModX, and Joomla all support search engine friendly URLs, though they use different names for the feature.

How can a business use competitor keywords in SEO without being deceptive?

The episode recommends creating a legitimate comparison page that names competitor products alongside your own, as demonstrated by a client called Bins from Columbus, Ohio comparing their service to Pods. This gives you a relevant, keyword-rich page without keyword stuffing, and keeps the user experience honest.

What is a tag cloud and why does it matter for a blog?

A tag cloud is a grouped display of keyword tags from blog posts, usually shown in a corner of the site. More frequently used tags appear larger. Clicking a tag shows all posts with that tag. WordPress offers an automatic widget for it, making it a quick SEO-friendly addition to any blog.

Terms defined in this episode
Google Boost
A Google product tested in Houston, Frisco, and other cities that allows businesses to run AdWords ads within local Google Places results, with no option for custom keyword selection.
Canonical tag
A tag used to tell Google which version of a URL is the preferred one, preventing duplicate content issues. The hosts describe it as related to 301 redirects.
SCADs
A term coined by the website Alliance Against Bait and Click, standing for scam ads, meaning pay-per-click ads that mention one brand but lead users to a competing product or unrelated page.
RSS feed
Real Simple Syndication, a feature that lets subscribers receive new blog posts or site updates automatically without visiting the site, described in the episode as force feeding content to readers.
