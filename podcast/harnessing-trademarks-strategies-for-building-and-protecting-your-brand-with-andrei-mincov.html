Questions this episode answers

How much does it cost to register a trademark versus going to court without one?

According to Andrei Mincov, getting a trademark registered costs anywhere from one to five thousand dollars. Litigating a dispute over an unprotected brand costs around 150,000 dollars. The registered trademark also makes most disputes resolve without court, because infringers are advised by their own lawyers to back down.

Does owning a trademark help with Google Ads and paid traffic?

Yes. Andrei Mincov explains that Google assigns lower CPC to well-known brands because Google trusts that clicks will result in good experiences. A trademarked, recognized brand also converts at higher rates and can command a higher average order value, improving all three key marketing metrics.

What happens if two businesses are already using the same name and neither has a trademark?

Mincov says there is sometimes no good answer. Trademark Factory first searches to see if a brand is even trademarkable. If another party already has rights, the best option may be to rebrand entirely, because promoting a name you cannot own means building someone else's brand that can always be taken away.

Does TikTok require a trademark to advertise on its platform?

Yes, according to Mincov. TikTok advertising requires a trademark because TikTok does not want to be in a situation where it is promoting something that does not belong to the advertiser. Google and Facebook similarly give priority to advertisers who can prove brand legitimacy.

Terms defined in this episode
Rebuttable presumption
A legal term Mincov uses to describe what a trademark registration provides: the presumption that your trademark is a valid brand and that you are the owner of it, which the other party must then disprove.
Common law passing off
The pre-trademark-law doctrine where a business could sue a copycat for passing off their products as someone else's, requiring extensive evidence before a judge with no formal registry to rely on.
Likelihood of confusion
The trademark standard Mincov describes where protection extends beyond identical names to all lookalikes, soundalikes, and meaning-alikes, including synonyms and translations into other languages.
Brand valuation
An assessment of how much of a company's total value comes from its brand. Mincov cites Nike as an example where the brand accounts for 47 out of 97 billion dollars in total company valuation.
