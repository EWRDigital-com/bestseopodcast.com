Questions this episode answers

What is indexation analysis in SEO and why does it matter?

Indexation analysis checks whether Google has actually added your pages to its index, not just crawled them. Pages with thin content, templated content, broken schema, or dynamically generated content may be crawled but never indexed, meaning they will not appear in any search results no matter how specific the query.

What is keyword cannibalization and how does it hurt SEO?

Keyword cannibalization occurs when multiple pages on the same site target the same topic or phrase, causing them to compete against each other. As competition for those phrases grows, the pages can undercut each other's ranking potential. A siloing strategy, pointing internal links up to one target page, can address this.

Should I use AMP on my WordPress site for SEO?

Results are mixed. Guest expert Loren Baker noted many companies wasted resources on AMP and it was detrimental. The hosts report their SEO team tried AMP plugins in WordPress and ultimately turned them off for SEO purposes. AMP is most clearly valuable for sites that primarily publish information or online publications.

How can on-site search data inform business decisions beyond SEO?

Tracking what visitors search for on your site reveals unmet demand. In the episode's example, a product called Folding Chicken Roaster was hard to find because customers searched for beer can chicken holder instead. That search data can guide product naming, page content, and even decisions about new product lines or locations.

Terms defined in this episode
Indexation Analysis
The process of comparing all pages on a site against the pages Google has actually indexed, to find pages that are crawled but never entered into the search index.
Keyword Cannibalization
A situation where multiple pages on the same site compete for the same search phrase, potentially reducing the ranking potential of all those pages.
AMP (Accelerated Mobile Pages)
A technology that strips JavaScript to make pages load faster on mobile devices, aimed at improving user experience and supporting Google's mobile-first indexing initiative.
Progressive Web App (PWA)
A newer web technology that downloads app-like functionality on the first page visit so repeat server requests are minimized, giving users a faster, app-style experience without visiting an app store.
