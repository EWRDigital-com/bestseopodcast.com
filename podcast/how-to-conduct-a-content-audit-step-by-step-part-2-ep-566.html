How To Conduct A Content Audit Step-By-Step (Part 2) Ep. 566

Ep. 56636 min2023-03-133,546 plays

A content audit tracks metrics like bounce rate, backlinks, time on page, and conversions to reveal what content works, then informs a refreshed strategy that deletes, rewrites, or repurposes underperforming pages.

Key takeaways
  • A bounce rate above 80% is a signal to revisit content and rethink the user flow.
  • Track backlinks over time because losing one high-quality backlink can tank rankings more than anything else.
  • Backlink quality matters more than quantity, niche-specific links outperform links from all-category sites.
  • Pages per session is a key metric, aim for two to three pages per session per visitor.
  • Audit content often because what works today may not work tomorrow and top pieces can quickly become outdated.

About this episode

Your content is what draws visitors to your website. Is yours pulling its weight? Perform a content audit to get the most from yours.

Questions this episode answers

What is a good bounce rate for websites?

According to the episode, a range of 26 to 45% is average for retail and e-commerce sites, B2B sites fall into the 25 to 55% range, and for blogs the number can be as high as 90%. A bounce rate above 80% is a signal to revisit that content.

Why should you track backlinks during a content audit?

Backlinks change over time and not all are good. A page might accumulate hundreds of backlinks but many could be spam or low quality. Losing a high-quality backlink can tank rankings, so monitoring the profile and recovering lost quality links is critical.

What actions should you take after completing a content audit?

Add a column to your spreadsheet indicating what action each piece needs: deleting, refreshing, rewriting, reusing, or just tracking it. Use the findings to develop a new content strategy with specific goals, informed by what topics and formats performed best.

How many pages per session should you target for content performance?

The hosts recommend targeting two to three pages per session per visitor as a starting goal. If users land on one page and leave immediately, the site is not offering the right solution and Google will recognize that users returned to search results unsatisfied.

Terms defined in this episode
Bounce Rate
The percentage of website visitors who arrive on a page and exit without engaging further. A range of 26 to 45% is average for retail and e-commerce, while blogs can see rates as high as 90%.
Homogeneous Backlink Profile
A backlink profile where links come from niche-relevant, consistent sources rather than all-category sites, making the link pattern look natural and useful to search engines.
UTM Parameters
Custom tracking codes added to URLs that help identify exactly where content traffic is coming from, used because Google does not always provide all traffic source data on its own.
Pages Per Session
A metric tracking how many pages a user views after landing on a piece of content, indicating whether the site is successfully guiding visitors deeper into the site.
