8 Big SEO Changes over the last Decade by Jason DeMers #346

Ep. 34636 min2016-11-08

SEO has shifted dramatically over 10 years, moving from keyword stuffing and link schemes to content quality, semantic search, mobile prioritization, and continuously running core algorithms like Panda and Penguin.

Key takeaways
  • The Panda update in 2011 ended spammy content and keyword stuffing, making quality content the central SEO focus.
  • Penguin becoming part of the core algorithm means paid or swapped links are always being evaluated, not just during updates.
  • Hummingbird introduced semantic search in 2013, shifting focus from keyword density to searcher intent.
  • Mobile internet usage surpassed desktop at 51.3% in October 2016, making mobile-friendly, fast-loading sites required.
  • Google's major algorithm updates are now continuous and part of the core, replacing random major rollouts that forced sudden fixes.

Questions this episode answers

What are the biggest ways SEO has changed in the last 10 years?

According to Jason Demers via Search Engine Land, the eight biggest changes are the rise of content, the death of link schemes, the reshaping of local search, SERP overhauls, the rise of the knowledge graph, mobile prioritization, the death of keyword stuffing via semantic search, and the shift to continuous core algorithm updates instead of random major rollouts.

Why does SEO require ongoing monthly work instead of a one-time fix?

Competitors continue making changes that can push your rankings down, and Google constantly updates its algorithm and SERPs. Panda and Penguin now run continuously as part of the core algorithm, so sites must keep content relevant and link profiles clean at all times.

What did the Hummingbird update change about SEO?

Hummingbird, released in 2013, introduced semantic search. It shifted SEO focus from matching exact keywords and keyword density to understanding searcher intent, meaning Google tries to determine what the user actually means and serves results accordingly, even without exact keyword matches.

How has the knowledge graph affected website traffic?

The knowledge graph gives users direct answers on the search results page, so they often do not need to click through to a website. This means well-optimized sites can lose traffic even when their content is selected as the best answer, because Google surfaces it without a click.

Terms defined in this episode
Semantic search
An approach introduced by the Hummingbird update in 2013 that focuses on the intent behind a search query rather than exact keyword matching, so Google returns results based on what the searcher means.
Knowledge graph
A Google feature that presents direct, concise answers to queries in a box on the results page, often preventing users from needing to click through to any website.
AMP (Accelerated Mobile Pages)
A framework backed by Google and Facebook that strips pages down to load very quickly on mobile devices, initially as plain content pages but now supporting forms and media.
Link scheme
Any deliberate attempt to artificially influence search rankings through links, such as buying links or swapping them, practices that Penguin as part of the core algorithm now continuously penalizes.
