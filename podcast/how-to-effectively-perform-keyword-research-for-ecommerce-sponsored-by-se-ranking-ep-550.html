Key takeaways
  • Target keywords above 20 monthly searches, but ecommerce sites often need thousands of monthly searches to drive meaningful revenue.
  • Spread rankings across many pages rather than depending on one high-volume keyword, since losing a single top position can devastate traffic.
  • Match content to all four search intent types: informational, navigational, commercial, and transactional, to cover the full conversion funnel.
  • Spy on competitors to find the keywords, backlinks, and content gaps you can fill with higher-quality pages to outrank them.

Questions this episode answers

What is a good minimum search volume for ecommerce keywords?

The episode states there is no magic number, but a basic rule of thumb is that anything over 20 monthly searches is worth considering. For active ecommerce sites, the hosts target keywords in the range of 1,000 to 10,000 monthly searches, depending on competition and lead value.

What are the four types of search intent for ecommerce SEO?

According to Think with Google as cited in the episode, the four types are informational (I want to know), navigational (I want to go), commercial (I want to do or compare), and transactional (I want to buy). Each type requires different keyword targeting and content strategy.

Why is keyword difficulty important for new ecommerce websites?

Keyword difficulty is scored from zero to 100 and estimates how hard it would be to rank on Google. The episode recommends new websites stay on the lower end of the scale, because it becomes exponentially harder to rank as difficulty approaches 100.

How can ecommerce businesses spy on competitor keywords?

The episode recommends checking which specific keywords competitors target, how many backlinks they have, where those backlinks come from, and what content topics they cover. Tools like SE Ranking can show organic search volume, paid search volume, referring domains, and traffic by geographic channel.

Terms defined in this episode
Keyword Difficulty
A score between zero and 100 that estimates how difficult it would be to rank on Google for a given keyword, with higher scores being exponentially harder to achieve.
Search Intent
The purpose behind a user's search query, categorized into informational, navigational, commercial, and transactional types, each reflecting a different stage of the buyer's journey.
Featured Snippet (Position Zero)
A search result that appears above standard organic results on Google, which informational content has a chance to occupy for target keywords.
Long-Tail Keywords
More specific, lower-volume search phrases that attract users who know exactly what they want, resulting in higher purchase intent compared to broad seed keywords.
