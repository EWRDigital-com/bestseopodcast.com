Key takeaways
  • Run many small one-off influencer deals first, then build a long-term family from those who perform and align with your brand.
  • A single influencer deal typically grants six months of rights to repurpose content across email, paid ads, Amazon, and social channels.
  • Set one clear goal before spending, whether eyeballs, content, or credibility, and do not change that goal after the campaign runs.
  • College athletes have been able to monetize since July 2021, making them an affordable option especially for local and regional businesses.
  • Micro and product-only deals starting at free product or around 200 to 300 dollars let brands test influencer fit before scaling budgets.

Questions this episode answers

How does Open Sponsorship work for brands wanting to partner with athletes?

Open Sponsorship started as a self-service marketplace where brands post campaigns and athletes or their agents apply. It has since added a services layer, acting as a tech-enabled agency that helps brands execute deals, manage contracts and payments, and amplify content across email, paid ads, PR, websites, and Amazon.

When is a business ready to start influencer marketing?

According to Ishveen Jolly, influencer marketing can start as early as product launch to gather user feedback and testimonials. It is most effective once all marketing channels are active so one deal can slot across email, paid social, and paid ads simultaneously, but even small companies can benefit early.

How should brands evaluate which influencers to work with?

Key data points include engagement rate, follower size, and demographic data. Open Sponsorship also uses a social listening tool to see who has already talked about a brand or segment and with what engagement. Beyond data, brands should assess video quality, content vibe, and whether they simply like the influencer's feed.

What budget should a brand expect to spend on influencer marketing?

Deals can start with free product or around 200 to 300 dollars for micro influencers, and brands can begin testing at roughly 5k per month. Ishveen Jolly notes that at lower spend levels the realistic return is content for paid ads, not direct sales, and budgets can scale up as results are proven.

Terms defined in this episode
Name, Image, and Likeness (NIL)
Rights that allow college athletes in the US to monetize themselves, permitted since July 2021, enabling brands to sign them for social posts, appearances, and content deals similar to professional athletes.
Always-on approach
A continuous influencer marketing strategy where a brand maintains active deals year-round, flexing spend up during peak periods like Amazon Prime Day and reducing to product-only deals during slower times.
Social listening tool
A platform feature used by Open Sponsorship to identify which influencers have previously discussed a brand or topic, and to measure the engagement those posts received before a deal is signed.
Repurposing rights
Contract terms, typically covering six months, that allow a brand to reuse an influencer's content in paid ads, email, Amazon listings, websites, and LinkedIn after the original post goes live.
