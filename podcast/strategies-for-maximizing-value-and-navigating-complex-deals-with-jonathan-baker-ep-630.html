Questions this episode answers

What do buyers look for when acquiring a marketing agency?

Buyers prioritize profitability first, then niche positioning, a business not heavily dependent on the founder, a strong business development process, and a balanced client portfolio without one dominant client. These factors determine both whether a deal happens and how the price is negotiated.

How does an earnout work in an agency acquisition?

In a typical earnout, a seller receives only a percentage of the total price upfront, with the rest contingent on hitting revenue targets and staying with the acquiring firm for two to three years or longer. Missing targets can mean losing part or all of the remaining payout.

What is the fee revenue per full-time employee benchmark for marketing agencies?

According to Jonathan Baker, many agencies get stuck at around 160,000 dollars in fee revenue per full-time employee. Firms that position well can reach 220,000 dollars or higher. Utilization targets should aim for 60 percent, while the average observed is 42 percent.

When should a marketing agency owner consider selling versus continuing to build?

Jonathan Baker suggests selling when your goal shifts from building to hitting a specific number, when you sense burnout approaching, or when further growth would require a costly new layer of management that would crush margins. If you are still enjoying the build, keep building.

Terms defined in this episode
Earnout
A deal structure where a seller receives a portion of the sale price upfront and the remainder contingent on hitting certain targets, typically over two to three years while staying with the acquiring firm.
Client concentration
The degree to which a firm's revenue depends on a small number of clients. High concentration is a red flag for buyers because losing one large client can be catastrophic.
Fee revenue per full-time employee
A benchmark metric dividing total fee income by total employee count, used to assess agency efficiency. Top-performing firms reach 220,000 dollars or higher according to Jonathan Baker.
Utilization rate
A measure of how well all hours worked across an entire firm, not just billable staff, convert into dollars at the firm's stated hourly rate. A target of 60 percent is cited, against an observed average of 42 percent.
