Questions this episode answers

What is the Google Panda update and how did it affect small businesses?

Google Panda, also called Farmer Panda, was a 2011 algorithm change focused on content quality, authority, and trust. It impacted 14% of U.S. search queries by April 2011. One online furniture retailer saw Google traffic drop 64%, and another company shrank from 17 staff to 5 due to a 40% sales decline.

What should online retailers do to recover from duplicate content penalties after Panda?

Retailers should hire freelance writers to create original, detailed product descriptions instead of using manufacturer copy. They should also add canonical tags or 301 redirects to consolidate duplicate URLs, ensuring Google credits the correct version of a page and does not index near-identical content.

What is the difference between a canonical tag and a 301 redirect?

A 301 redirect is the preferred method according to Matt Cutts and should be used when accessible. A canonical tag is the fallback option for situations where 301 redirects cannot be implemented, such as on templated free websites. Both tell search engines which URL version is authoritative.

Why should SEO strategies include pay-per-click and social media and not just organic search?

Google can change its algorithm at any time with no legal liability, as seen when Panda wiped out traffic for sites dependent on organic listings. Pay-per-click listings are more stable because, as the hosts note, natural listings will disappear before paid listings do, making diversification essential.

Terms defined in this episode
Google Panda (Farmer Panda)
A 2011 Google algorithm update focused on content quality, authority, trust, and user signals that penalized duplicate, low-quality, and farmed content and impacted 14% of U.S. search queries.
Canonical tag
An HTML tag placed on non-target pages to tell search engines which URL is the authoritative version, used when 301 redirects are not available.
Absolute link
An internal link that contains the full URL including the domain, so that if content is scraped and reposted, the link still points back to the original site.
Google Caffeine
A 2010 Google indexing infrastructure change, not an algorithm update, that gave Google the ability to index over 100,000 gigabytes of content per day using 100 million gigabytes of storage.
