Geofencing works best for retailers when ads are placed where customers already are, kept brief with a clear call to action, timed to business hours, and built on transparent opt-in consent.

Key takeaways
  • Build geofences where customers already spend time, not just near your own store.
  • Geofence size should keep users within five minutes of traveling time to stay relevant.
  • Action-driven, location-specific messages with scarcity or reciprocity outperform plain ads.
  • Time geofenced promotions to match open business hours and peak conversion windows.
  • Two types exist: real-time geofencing for leads and historical data lookup going back two years.

Questions this episode answers

What is a geofence and how does it work for retail advertising?

A geofence is a virtual perimeter around a physical space such as a retail location or a bus stop. Marketers send text or push notifications to users who enter that perimeter, but users must opt in, typically through an app. Burger King used this with their Whopper Detour campaign, triggering offers when customers were near a McDonald's.

How big should a geofence be for a retail campaign?

According to the article discussed in this episode, the perimeter should be within five minutes of traveling time to the target location. Anything longer reduces the relevancy of local messaging and makes the offer less useful to the nearby consumer.

What are the two main types of geofencing for advertisers?

The first is real-time geofencing, where ads are served to users in the moment as they cross a virtual boundary. These impressions are more expensive but better for leads. The second uses historical location data, which can go back two years, to build audience segments from past events or competitor locations at a lower cost per impression.

Why is timing important in a geofencing campaign?

Sending a store offer to nearby consumers during off business hours does not make sense and can frustrate potential customers. Hosts recommend using Google Analytics to find peak conversion windows, for example one client saw the most conversions Wednesday and Thursday from 10 to 12, and concentrating budget in those windows.

Terms defined in this episode
Geofence
A virtual perimeter around a physical space such as a retail location or a bus stop, used to trigger mobile ads or push notifications when a user enters the area.
Beacon
A physical device placed at a specific location, such as inside a building, used to track foot traffic and deliver targeted ads to nearby devices.
Real-time geofencing
Geofencing that serves impressions to users the moment they cross a virtual boundary; typically more expensive but better for generating leads.
Historical geofencing
A method of building ad audiences using location data from past events or competitor sites, which can go back two years, at a lower cost per impression than real-time targeting.
