The Beginners Guide to Landing Pages #433

Ep. 43359 min2018-08-20

A well-designed landing page converts better when it has one clear goal, minimal form fields, a benefit-focused headline, mobile optimization, and a call-to-action button that tells visitors exactly what they get.

Apple PodcastsSpotifyYouTubeiHeartRadioRSS
Key takeaways
  • Keep landing page form fields to the minimum needed, as each extra field creates friction and reduces conversions.
  • Use button text that states what the visitor receives, such as 'Send me my free report,' not 'Submit' or 'Click here.'
  • Check analytics for mobile vs. desktop traffic split before finalizing your landing page design and layout.
  • Match your ad headline to your landing page headline so the message is congruent all the way through.
  • Offer a free guide or report instead of a free consultation to lower the barrier to entry and capture more leads.

Questions this episode answers

How many form fields should a landing page have?

Ask only for the information you need, as little as possible. The hosts note that reducing fields to just email can triple or quadruple leads. Each extra field creates roughly 23% friction. Additional information can be collected over the phone or in a follow-up email after the lead is captured.

Why do long-form sales letters outperform short landing pages for some products?

Short copy often fails to answer authority, credibility, and trust questions or overcome objections. Long-form sales letters are structured so even skimmers absorb key bullets and build enough trust to convert. They are especially effective for info products and high-ticket items.

How should you split test a landing page?

Start with the largest elements: the headline, the call-to-action, and whether or not a video is present. Small design changes like background color shifts matter much less. Use an A/B split so some traffic sees page A and some sees page B, and measure the impact before changing anything else.

Terms defined in this episode
Squeeze page
A landing page focused solely on capturing a visitor's contact information, typically by offering something of value like a free guide or report in exchange.
Above the fold
The portion of a landing page visible without scrolling. The hosts stress placing the headline, key message, and lead capture form in this area to maximize conversions.
Long-form sales letter
A lengthy landing page or video sales letter designed to answer every objection and wipe away doubt, commonly used for info products and affiliate marketing offers.
WIIFM
Stands for what's in it for me. The hosts use this term to mean copy should focus on benefits to the visitor, not the company's features or awards.
