6 Ways to Make your SEO More Authoritiative by Jason DeMers #337

Ep. 33733 min2016-09-14

Writing authoritative SEO content requires citing credentials, using real facts and examples, including images and video, linking to outside sources, and making yourself available for reader questions.

Apple PodcastsSpotifyYouTubeiHeartRadioRSS
Key takeaways
  • Write from a professional perspective by referencing your title, credentials, or measurable experience in the content itself.
  • Align your brand with recognized manufacturers or industry names to transfer their authority to your content.
  • Include images and video to boost credibility, but balance them with enough written content to avoid overcrowding.
  • Cite real facts and statistics, such as local data or industry figures, to position yourself as an expert source.
  • After publishing, make yourself available for questions in comments or on social media to reinforce your expert status.
when you publish authoritative content, that's the first step into becoming that expert

About this episode

What is authoritativeness? How can you use it to improve your content and SEO? Chris and Charles will tell you as they review this week's article, "6 Ways to make your content more authoritative for SEO" by Jason DeMers posted at Search Engine Guide.

Questions this episode answers

How do you make SEO content more authoritative?

According to this episode, there are six ways: write from a professional perspective with credentials, use personal or manufacturer brands, include images and videos, cite real examples and facts, link to outside authoritative sources, and make yourself available for questions after publishing.

How can a small business without many writers use personal branding for SEO?

The hosts suggest that instead of relying on individual writer brands, small businesses should align themselves with a well-known manufacturer or supplier brand, especially if they hold a unique certification or exclusive status, to borrow authority from that larger brand.

Terms defined in this episode
Authoritativeness
The quality of content that demonstrates expertise through credentials, real facts, outbound links to known authorities, and professional perspective, which Google, Yahoo, and Bing use to evaluate content quality.
Schema markup for reviews
Structured data applied to reviews on a website. Google now requires that schema markup be used only on reviews produced directly by the website, not on reviews syndicated from third-party platforms like Yelp or Angie's List.
Shiko
A term used by the hosts meaning a share, a like, or a follow on any social media platform.
AlgoCat
Short for algorithm cataclysm, a term the hosts use to describe a major Google algorithm update or significant change to search guidelines.
