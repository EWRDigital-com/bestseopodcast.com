Questions this episode answers

Why do LLMs cite pages that rank outside the top 10 in Google?

According to Krešimir Ćorluka, pages ranked 20th or 50th often explain a topic more completely than top-ranked pages. A lower-ranked page may lack strong backlinks or brand authority but have highly contextual content, original insights, data, or statistics that an LLM finds more useful to cite.

Is SEO dying because of AI search?

The guest argues that bad SEO is dying, meaning cloaking, keyword stuffing, and low-quality long articles. Good SEO built on fundamentals is producing strong results for clients and is essentially the same practice as LLM optimization. Whoever does their job right still gets listed in LLMs.

How should SEO agencies handle rank tracking and attribution with AI search?

Krešimir Ćorluka recommends asking clients directly what KPI they want to track rather than building complex reports. Detailed reports often conflict with other tools because SERPs are personalized. For LLMs, he suggests treating visibility like a billboard channel rather than a direct traffic channel.

What skills do SEOs need beyond traditional optimization to succeed in the AI era?

The hosts agree SEOs need to understand how LLMs are trained, how RAG works, customer journey mapping, conversion rate optimization, digital PR, PPC, and basic web development concepts. A checklist approach without this broader framework breaks down when edge cases arise or results do not match expectations.

Terms defined in this episode
LLM EO / AEO
Terms used in the episode for optimizing content so that large language models surface it in AI-generated answers, described by the guest as essentially the same practice as good SEO.
RAG
Retrieval-Augmented Generation, referenced in the episode as the method LLMs use to pull external information when generating answers, which early LLMs relied on Google to supply.
Digital PR
A creative outreach and content strategy aimed at earning media coverage and links, described in the episode as a distinct skill set that requires understanding why people read content, not just how to write articles.
AI slop
A term used in the episode to describe low-quality AI-generated content flooding platforms like YouTube and social networks, which the guest predicts could push audiences back toward offline attention.
