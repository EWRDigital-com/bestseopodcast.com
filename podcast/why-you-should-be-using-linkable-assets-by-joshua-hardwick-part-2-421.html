Why You Should Be Using Linkable Assets by Joshua Hardwick Part 2 #421

Ep. 42126 min2018-05-29

Linkable assets such as awards and rankings, studies, and original research attract backlinks by giving others a reason to share and cite your content, with outreach and paid promotion amplifying results.

Key takeaways
  • Awards and rankings pages earn links naturally; contact everyone featured on the list to prompt sharing.
  • Creating a badge others can display on their sites generates backlinks with minimal effort.
  • Updating outdated studies, like the Moz 2015 ranking factors survey, lets you reclaim existing link equity.
  • Putting a paid budget behind linkable assets, such as targeted Facebook ads, accelerates link building.
  • Finding people already discussing your research topic and pitching your study directly is an effective outreach method.

Questions this episode answers

How do you build links to an awards or rankings page?

Reach out directly to everyone featured on the list or who won an award, since they will want to share it. You can also target fans of those winners with paid Facebook ads, showing ads to people who have liked the featured people's pages.

How can you use outdated studies for link building?

Find a well-known but outdated study, such as the Moz search engine ranking factors study from 2015, recreate or update it with fresh data, and then earn the backlinks that previously pointed to the old version by offering a more current resource.

Terms defined in this episode
Linkable asset
A piece of content created specifically because it earns backlinks, such as awards lists, rankings, infographics, online tools, or original research studies.
Link juice
The ranking value or authority passed from one page to another through a hyperlink, referenced in the context of badges and high domain authority sites.
Domain authority
A score indicating a website's strength for ranking purposes; the hosts note that links from sites with authority above 30 or 50 are more valuable than lower-authority sites.
Skyscraper technique (implied: steal their links)
Finding popular but outdated studies or content, recreating them with updated data, and then earning the backlinks that previously pointed to the older version.
