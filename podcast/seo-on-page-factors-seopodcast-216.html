Strong on-page SEO in 2014 requires quality content, conversational keyword thinking, proper HTML titles and meta descriptions, structured data markup, fast site speed, and mobile-optimized responsive design.

Apple PodcastsSpotifyYouTubeiHeartRadioRSS
Key takeaways
  • Write content that can stand on its own as a topic, or combine thin pages rather than publishing short fluff articles.
  • Think in conversational phrases and long-tail queries when writing content, not just target keywords.
  • Titles should be 50 to 70 characters; meta descriptions must be unique per page and include a call to action.
  • Clear your cache and load your site from a local cafe on public Wi-Fi to honestly test page speed.
  • Responsive design alone may not be enough for mobile users who primarily need a phone number and address.

Questions this episode answers

How long should a page title be for SEO in 2014?

According to the episode, the optimum title length is between 50 and 70 characters. Exceeding that limit risks Google rewriting your title in the search results. You should focus on one keyword and optionally include your brand name separated by a pipe or hyphen.

Should small business websites run display ads for SEO and conversions?

The hosts advise against ads for service-based businesses like plumbers, law offices, or medical practices. Ads are described as a distraction that takes visitors away from the site. Sites whose revenue depends entirely on traffic and ads are the exception where running ads makes sense.

How does content freshness affect SEO rankings?

The episode recommends having an editorial calendar so content is created strategically and consistently. Stale content does not perform well in search. Fresh content should also be unique, not just timely, because covering the same topic with a distinct angle provides more value than similar articles.

When should you use structured data markup on a website?

Structured data markup is recommended when you sell products, run regular events, or have a physical location you want visitors to find. The episode notes it can display business hours, addresses, and event details directly in search results, though site owners should weigh that against driving clicks to the full website.

Terms defined in this episode
Content engagement
Whether visitors spend time reading content or bounce quickly away, including actions like filling out forms, clicking links, watching videos, or sharing on social media.
Structured data markup
Code added to a website that allows Google to display additional details like business hours, addresses, events, or product information directly in search results.
Editorial calendar
A planning tool that helps site owners create and post content strategically and consistently to avoid stale content in search.
Responsive web design
A design approach where a single set of URLs serves all devices and the layout adjusts to fit different screen sizes, recommended by both Google and Bing as the ideal configuration.
