Questions this episode answers

What are the three types of goals in Google Analytics covered in this episode?

The episode covers time-based goals, which track how long visitors stay on a site; page view-based goals, which trigger when a visitor exceeds a set number of page views; and URL destination goals, which fire when a visitor reaches a specific page such as a purchase thank-you screen.

Why did Google increase the number of Analytics goals from 4 to 20?

The hosts say Google raising the limit signals that four goals were not enough for most businesses. Previously users had to create duplicate accounts to track more than four goals, so the increase to 20 makes the tool far more practical for tracking multiple conversion types.

How can time-based Google Analytics goals improve a pay-per-click campaign?

By seeing which keywords bring visitors who stay on the site for longer periods, advertisers can shift budget toward those higher-quality keywords. If certain keywords consistently produce short visits, the corresponding landing pages can be revised to better match what those visitors are searching for.

What is a URL destination goal and when should you use it?

A URL destination goal is triggered when a visitor reaches a specific page, such as an e-commerce thank-you page after a purchase. The hosts recommend pairing it with Google's e-commerce code so the goal also records how much the customer spent, linking revenue directly to keywords and traffic paths.

Terms defined in this episode
URL destination goal
A Google Analytics goal type that fires when a visitor reaches a specific URL, commonly a thank-you or order-confirmation page, indicating a completed conversion.
time-based goal
A Google Analytics goal that tracks how long a visitor stays on a site, allowing marketers to identify which keywords or pages hold attention longest.
page view-based goal
A Google Analytics goal triggered when a visitor exceeds a set number of page views, used to measure how deeply visitors engage with a site's content.
funnel
A multi-step path in Google Analytics Goals, up to 10 steps, that tracks how many visitors enter and complete a defined sequence of pages, mirroring a classic sales process.
