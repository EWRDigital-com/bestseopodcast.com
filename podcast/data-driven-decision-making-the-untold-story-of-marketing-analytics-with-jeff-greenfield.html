Questions this episode answers

What is ad stock or carryover in marketing analytics?

Ad stock, also called carryover, is the phenomenon where an ad's effect does not stop when the ad ends. After a Super Bowl ad, for example, web traffic stays elevated for at least seven to ten days before gradually declining. Every ad type, from pay-per-click to CTV, has its own ad stock curve and time to conversion.

Why is last-click attribution still common at large companies if it is inaccurate?

According to Jeff Greenfield, large companies often remain on last-click attribution not because it is correct but because consistency matters more than accuracy in a corporate setting. A consistently wrong model is easier to defend internally than switching to a new model that may show different numbers.

How can a marketer use impression data to find what is really driving organic traffic?

Greenfield recommends downloading daily impression data from every ad platform for the past 12 months, then graphing impressions, clicks, time on site, and sales together. When time on site and sales spike, look back a couple of weeks in the impression data to identify which campaign created the awareness that eventually drove those organic visits.

Terms defined in this episode
Incrementality
The measure of additional sales that are directly caused by advertising, separating them from purchases that would have happened anyway without any ad spend.
Ad Stock (Carryover)
The lingering effect of an advertisement after it stops running, where awareness and sales impact decay gradually over days or weeks rather than stopping immediately.
Marginal Response Curve
The relationship showing that each additional dollar spent on a channel produces diminishing returns, eventually reaching a saturation point where ROI declines with more spend.
