10 Valuable Content Marketing Lessons From Print by Julia McCoy #325

Ep. 32535 min2016-06-20

Good online content borrows from print by prioritizing research, teaching readers something new, maintaining authority, checking facts, and breaking long pieces into digestible chunks that match a brand's consistent tone.

Apple PodcastsSpotifyYouTubeiHeartRadioRSS
Key takeaways
  • Do your research before writing; one incorrect fact destroys an article's credibility instantly.
  • Teach readers something new so they leave with value and are more likely to share or engage.
  • Fact-check everything, including sources you cite, to avoid spreading other people's bad facts.
  • Break long content into multiple posts, then combine them into a case study or white paper later.
  • Diversify content based on your industry position; a single-brand site needs consistent tone, not wide variety.

About this episode

What made print so attractive back in the day, and how can you apply what worked in that era to your content marketing today? Your BestSEOPodcast hosts are going to talk about that on this episode featuring an article written by Julia McCoy.

Questions this episode answers

What are the most important content marketing lessons from print media?

According to this episode, the top lessons are: do your research, seek to teach, write with a unique objective viewpoint, master your craft through editing, be an authority, use specific facts and figures, fact-check everything, aim for emotional depth, break content into digestible pieces, and diversify based on your industry position.

Why should online content writers fact-check their sources?

The episode warns that if you rely on a source that did not fact-check its own content, you republish their errors and damage your own credibility. Building authority depends on readers trusting that your facts are always accurate and well-researched.

When should a business diversify its content versus keeping a consistent tone?

The episode says it depends on industry position. A single-brand company like an AC repair firm should keep the same tone, look, and color scheme across all content. Larger brands covering many audiences, like a major manufacturer, benefit more from diverse viewpoints and voices.
