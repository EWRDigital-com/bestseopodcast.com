Questions this episode answers

What is Google's Local Sponsored Listing and how much does it cost?

Google's Local Sponsored Listing is a paid feature that adds a yellow highlighted sponsor label to a business's Google local map result to make it stand out. The cost is $25 per month. At the time of this episode it was being piloted in Houston and San Jose.

Is forwarding multiple domain names to one website considered black hat SEO?

Forwarding domains to a main website is not black hat, but Google will likely detect the forwarded domains as duplicate content and place them in the supplemental index. A better approach is to build separate sites with original content that link back to the main site.

What is the average click-through rate for Google local listings?

According to a Google sales representative discussed in this episode, Google local listings receive approximately a 5% click-through rate on average. The sponsored listing is designed to increase that rate, but the representative could not provide a specific number for the uplift.

Why should a website use a single Google Analytics profile instead of one profile per page?

Using one profile per page prevents goal tracking, such as measuring funnel completions or thank-you page visits, because navigating between pages registers as a bounce. A single profile for the whole site allows Google Analytics to follow users page by page and track goals properly.

Terms defined in this episode
Google Local Sponsored Listing
A paid Google feature that places a yellow highlighted label on a business's local map result to make it stand out from other local listings, priced at $25 per month.
Supplemental Index
A secondary Google index where pages with duplicate or low-value content are stored, making it unlikely those pages will appear prominently in search results.
Transactional website
A website oriented around completing purchases or transactions, as opposed to a content-driven site; the episode cites Amazon as an example.
Analytics goal
A Google Analytics setting that tracks a specific user action, such as reaching a thank-you page or completing a purchase funnel, which requires a single site-wide analytics profile to function correctly.
