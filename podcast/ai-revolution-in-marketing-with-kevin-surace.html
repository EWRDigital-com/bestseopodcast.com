Questions this episode answers

How can marketers get their brand recommended by AI systems like ChatGPT instead of relying on Google rankings?

According to Kevin Surace, you can ask an LLM directly what are some ways to have your company show up in its results versus competitors, and it will tell you. Traditional SEO backlink strategies do not control AI recommendations, so the approach to visibility must change entirely.

Is AI generated content bad for SEO and should companies ban it?

Kevin Surace says companies that ban AI content are making a critical mistake, comparing it to refusing to use the internet in 1995. He argues competitors using LLMs will produce better, faster, and more persuasive content, and that companies ignoring this will be overtaken by startups.

How fast can AI generate a full year of blog posts for a business?

Kevin Surace explains you can prompt a model to review your website, generate 52 topic ideas, and write a thousand words for each, all in under an hour. Editing and finalizing all 52 posts can be completed in a single day, which he calls at least a thousand X improvement.

What skills do marketing professionals need to stay relevant as AI takes over content creation?

Kevin Surace says marketers need to stop treating AI as a toy and use it in every workflow. The key shift is from being a doer to being a strategic partner who applies expert judgment to guide, prompt, and edit AI output rather than producing content manually.

Terms defined in this episode
Transformer model
A model architecture published by Google in 2017 that learns phrases rather than individual words, enabling AI to translate and later generate fluent natural language at scale.
AI Overview
A Google search feature that lets AI answer a search query directly on the results page, reducing the need for users to click through to any website.
Agentic AI
AI set up by a human to execute a defined series of tasks autonomously, such as writing and posting blog content on a scheduled workflow, but still initiated and controlled by the user.
EAT / EEAT
Google's quality framework covering expertise, experience, authority, and trust, used to weight content differently based on who is producing it, such as a doctor or lawyer versus an anonymous writer.
