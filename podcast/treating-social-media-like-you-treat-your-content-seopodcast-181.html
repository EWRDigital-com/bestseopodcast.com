Key takeaways
  • Sync social posting days with blog publishing days so audiences anticipate and look for your content.
  • Build anticipation before major content events by posting more frequently, up to three or four times a day that week.
  • Write for your audience using their language, not industry jargon, for example AC repair instead of HVAC.
  • Respond to every comment and question on social, adjusting speed and depth to match the platform norms.
  • Set up goal tracking in analytics to follow the path from each social post through to a purchase or conversion.
They have to run parallel. They have to really be congruent with each other.

Questions this episode answers

Why should my social media schedule match my content publishing schedule?

Posting on social the same days you publish content trains your audience to expect new material on those days. Over time they start looking for your tweets and Facebook posts on those days and are more likely to click and share, even before reading the article.

Should I curate other people's content when I have nothing original to share?

The hosts suggest skipping pure curation. Instead, link to the popular post for proper citation, then use your own sections to explain a different viewpoint or opinion on the same topic. This gives readers added value beyond what the original article offered.

How should I optimize social media posts for search?

Include your main key phrase in your Twitter bio and work it into posts where it fits naturally. The episode notes that with the exception of walled garden sites like Facebook, social posts can show up in web searches, so keyword placement matters.

How do I track whether a social media post is actually driving sales?

Set up a goal funnel in analytics that follows users from each social platform, through the post, to the product page and checkout. Post the same content on Twitter, Facebook, Google Plus, and YouTube, then compare referral data to see which channel converts best, and repeat what works.

Terms defined in this episode
walled garden
A site like Facebook where content stays inside the platform and does not surface in general web search results, limiting SEO benefit of posts made there.
content schedule
A planned calendar of publishing dates for blog posts or other content, which the episode recommends integrating with a matching social posting schedule.
WebP
An image format created by Google, described in the episode as similar to JPEG or PNG, that Google reported saved bandwidth without reducing image quality.
